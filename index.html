DSCI 304 Showcase Project

Analyzing Human Preference in AI Responses

This project analyzes the Anthropic Human Helpfulness and Harmlessness Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback (HH-RLHF) dataset, a publicly available dataset collected by Anthropic Labs to study human preferences in AI-generated responses. The dataset contains ~160,800 pairs of conversations between humans and an AI assistant, where each pair includes a chosen and a rejected response. The central research question driving this analysis is: what measurable text features, if any, distinguish responses that humans prefer from those they reject?

Figure 01

Response Length

This visualization examines whether users systematically preferred longer or shorter AI responses by plotting the distribution of word count differences between chosen and rejected responses for each prompt pair. The distribution follows a normal curve, with a mean difference of only 2.8 words. This finding challenges the assumption that more elaborate, detailed responses are inherently preferred, suggesting that users were capable of distinguishing response quality independent of length.

Figure 02

Word Overlap

This visualization compares the top 20 frequent words appearing in chosen versus rejected responses to determine whether the two response types draw from meaningfully different vocabularies. After removing stopwords and short filler words, 81.8% of the top 20 words were identical across both response types with only two words unique to each. This near-complete vocabulary overlap confirms that chosen and rejected responses are lexically indistinguishable at the surface level. This result strengthens the overall narrative that users were not responding to what words the AI used, but to something more subtle and harder to quantify.

Figure 03

Sentiment Analysis

This visualization scores the sentiment of each chosen and rejected last response, displaying the full distributions as violin plots with embedded boxplots. While the violin plots reveal that both distributions are centered slightly above zero, indicating generally positive tone across all responses, the near-identical shapes and means confirm that sentiment is not a meaningful driver of human preference. Users were not simply selecting responses that sounded warmer or more positive.

Figure 04

Sentiment by Conversation Depth

This visualization tracks mean sentiment scores for both chosen and rejected last responses across conversations with depths from 1 to 10. Both response types show a modest decline in sentiment with deeper conversations suggesting that longer conversations tend to involve more topics where neutral, measured language is more appropriate. The gap between chosen and rejected sentiment remains consistently narrow throughout all turn counts, indicating that conversation depth does not amplify or diminish the sentiment difference between preferred and non-preferred responses.

Figure 05

Response by Conversation Depth

This visualization plots the mean word count of the last assistant response across conversations with depths from 1 to 10, separately for chosen and rejected responses. Contrary to what might be expected, both response types get progressively shorter as conversations deepen, with mean word counts declining steadily from earlier to later turns. This pattern likely reflects conversational narrowing where as dialogue progresses, human questions become more specific and focused, cuasing shorter and more targeted responses. Again, chosen and rejected responses follow nearly identical trajectories throughout, further confirming that response length does not distinguish preferred from non-preferred AI responses.
